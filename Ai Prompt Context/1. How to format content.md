### **The Structure - How to Start, Build, and End**

#### **1. How to START (Don't Tell a Joke!)**

- **The Mistake:** Starting with a joke. People are still settling in and adjusting to your voice. It usually falls flat.
    
- **The Winning Move:** Start with an **"Empowerment Promise."**
    
    - _Example:_ "By the end of this talk, you will know something about speaking that you don't know now, and it might make a difference in your life."
        
    - This gives people a reason to listen.
        

#### **2. How to BUILD Your Idea (The Core Heuristics)**

Use these four techniques to make your ideas stick:

- **🔄 Heuristic 1: Cycle on Your Subject**
    
    - Tell them what you're going to tell them. Tell them. Then tell them what you told them.
        
    - **Why?** At any given time, 20% of your audience is zoned out. Repeating the core idea ensures everyone gets it.
        
- **🚧 Heuristic 2: Build a "Fence" Around Your Idea**
    
    - Distinguish your idea from similar ones so it isn't confused.
        
    - _Example:_ "My algorithm is similar to Jones's, but his is exponential, while mine is linear." Or, "This is an arch. This is _not_ an arch."
        
- **📍 Heuristic 3: Use Verbal Punctuation**
    
    - People's minds will wander. Give them clear landmarks to "get back on the bus."
        
    - _Example:_ "So, that was the first point on cycling. Now, let's move to the second point, building a fence..." This signals a seam in the talk.
        
- **❓ Heuristic 4: Ask a Question**
    
    - Poses a question to the audience to re-engage them.
        
    - **The 7-Second Rule:** You can wait up to 7 seconds for an answer—it will feel like an eternity, but it's the standard.
        
    - The question must be carefully chosen: not too obvious, not too hard.
        

#### **3. How to STOP (Leave a Lasting Impression)**

- **The Final Slide:** **Never** end with "Questions?", "Thank You!", or a long list of collaborators.
    
- **The Right Final Slide: "Contributions."**
    
    - Your last slide should be a clear, concise list of what you've contributed. It should mirror the "steps" you promised at the start. This is what stays on the screen during Q&A.
        
- **The Final Words: Don't Just Say "Thank You"**
    
    - "Thank you" suggests people stayed out of politeness, not interest.
        
    - **Better Ways to End:**
        
        - **A Salute to the Audience:** "It's been a privilege speaking to you today."
            
        - **A Benediction:** "God bless you, and God bless this institution." (See political speeches)
            
        - **A Joke:** People are now tuned in. A final joke makes them feel like they had fun the whole time.
            
    - Wait for the applause to start, _then_ you can mouth "thank you."

### **The Tools - Boards, Props, and Slides**

#### **📝 The Blackboard (The Best Tool for Informing)**

- **Why it works:**
    
    - **Graphic Quality:** You can easily use diagrams.
        
    - **Perfect Speed:** Your writing speed matches the audience's absorption speed.
        
    - **A Target for Your Hands:** Gives you something to do with your hands instead of putting them in your pockets (which is insulting in some cultures).
        

#### **🎪 Props (The Secret to Being Memorable)**

- **Why they work:** They create **"Empathetic Mirroring."** Your audience's mirror neurons fire, making them feel like _they_ are handling the object.
    
- **The Bicycle Wheel Example:** A spinning wheel demonstrates complex physics intuitively, making the lesson unforgettable.
    
- **The Pendulum & The Nose:** A dramatic demonstration of faith in the conservation of energy. (But don't try it at home!)
    

#### **📊 Slides (Use for Exposing, Not Teaching)**

- **The Cardinal Sins of Slides (and how to fix them):**
    
    - **Sin: Too Many Words.**
        
        - **Fix:** People can either read or listen. They will read. Use few words. **Font size should be 40pt or larger.**
            
    - **Sin: Clutter & Distraction.**
        
        - **Fix:** Remove logos, background junk, and excessive bullets. Simplify.
            
    - **Sin: The Laser Pointer.**
        
        - **Fix:** It breaks your connection with the audience. Instead, **embed arrows or circles** in your slide to guide the eye.
            
    - **Sin: The "Too-Heavy" Talk.**
        
        - **Fix:** Print your slides. Lay them on a table. If it looks like a "wall of text," it's too heavy. Add "air" and imagery.
            
- **The "Hapax Legomenon" Slide:**
    
    - This is your "crazy complex" slide that you can use **exactly once** per talk to demonstrate overwhelming complexity (e.g., a map of tribal alliances in Afghanistan). Its point is to be impossible to understand.

### **Special Cases - Job Talks & Getting Famous**

#### **💼 The Job Talk (The 5-Minute Test)**

You have **5 minutes** to convince a hiring committee of two things:

1. **You have a VISION.** (A problem that matters and a new approach.)
    
2. **You have DONE something.** (You've taken concrete steps toward that vision.)
    

**The Sandwich Method:**

- **Start:** Here is my vision and the steps needed to achieve it.
    
- **Middle:** This is what I did (blah, blah, blah).
    
- **End:** Here are my **contributions** (a direct mirror of the steps).
    

#### **⭐ How to Get Your Work Remembered (Winston's Star)**

Package your ideas with these 5 S's:

1. **Symbol:** A visual associated with your work (e.g., an arch).
    
2. **Slogan:** A memorable phrase (e.g., "One-Shot Learning").
    
3. **Surprise:** A counter-intuitive result (e.g., "You can learn from a single example.").
    
4. **Salient Idea:** One idea that sticks out, not a dozen competing ones (e.g., "The Power of the Near Miss").
    
5. **Story:** The narrative of how you did it and why it matters.
