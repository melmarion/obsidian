Paul (c. 50 AD)

The earliest account is in Paul's first letter to sect he founded in Corinth, written some time in the 50s AD: “For what I received I passed on to you as of first importance: that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, and then to the Twelve. Then he appeared to James, then to all the apostles, and last of all he appeared to me also, as to one abnormally born.” (1Cor 15:3-8)

  

The first thing worth noting here is the fact that Paul includes himself in his list of those to whom the risen Jesus "appeared." Both his references to his encountering the risen Jesus and the three (slightly different) descriptions of this encounter in Acts all make it clear that this was a vision - a light from heaven and a disembodied voice - not an encounter with the physical. The verb Paul uses for all these appearances he mentions is the same one - ὤφθη meaning "appeared, was seen" - in each case. He makes no distinction between the appearance of Jesus (عليه السلام) to him and the appearances to others. This idea was not universally accepted by all Jews, and it seems to have become disputed in the Corinthian community of the sect. Paul asks "if it is preached that Christ has been raised from the dead, how can some of you say that there is no resurrection of the dead?" (v. 12) and goes on to call Jesus' resurrection "the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep'' i.e. the prefigurement of the coming general resurrection. He goes on to address the question of whether this coming resurrection will involve the rising of physical bodies and says in response "How foolish!” Therefore, it is clear that for Paul, his rising did not involve a physical body. This is why Paul's references to and insistence on the fact of the rising of Jesus (عليه السلام) makes no mention of the evidence of a physical revivification of his dead body that features in some of the later accounts: the empty tomb, discarded grave cloths, people touching Jesus, Jesus (عليه السلام) eating and his physical form flying up into heaven. For Paul, at this early stage of the development of the story, the risen Jesus (عليه السلام) is a spiritual concept involving visions, not physical encounters.

After 70 AD, however, round stones became far more common. So this detail seems to be indicating the kind of tomb in the later First Century, given that a tomb of this style was exceedingly rare in Jesus' time. This could just be the writer of gMark indicating the kind of tomb in the time he was writing or it could be that the tomb itself, an element conspicuous by its absence in Paul's version, was an addition to the story.

The doctrine of the trinity cannot possibly be true from the perspective of classical logic. Logically, what does the belief in the Trinity entail? Trinitarians will all affirm the following premises:

Premise 1, the Father is God.

Premise 2, the Son is God.

Premise 3, the Holy Spirit is God.

Premise 4, the Father is not the Son.

Premise 5, the Father is not the Holy Spirit.

Premise 6, the Son is not the Holy Spirit.

Premise 7, there is exactly one God. 

  

Trinitarians affirm all seven of these premises. However, these premises form an inconsistent set. In other words, they cannot all be true. The basic problem here is tritheism. If the father, son, and holy spirit are not each other, yet they are all fully God, then this logically follows that there are three gods. Because of this problem, this then calls into question how we understand the statements the father is God, the son is God, or the holy spirit is God? And the key distinction is over. The phrase “is God” in all three of those premises. So there are a couple ways we can understand the phrase “is God.” It can either be an is of identity or an is of predication. If the phrase “is God” is meant to be a statement of identity, meaning that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are all numerically identical to God, then by classical identity it follows that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are all identical to each other, which is a heresy. It's known as modalism, and this is something Christians want to avoid.

What if we understand the phrase “is God” as an “is” of relative identity, meaning the father, son, and holy spirit are all identical to God, but they're not identical to each other. This avoids the heresy. However, this violates logic, the classical law of identity, and most people are reluctant to do so. And this cannot be considered a solution from the paradigm of classical logic. Okay, well, what if we analyze the phrase “is God” in terms of predication? Well, this isn't a solution either. If all three persons are ascribed with the quality of divinity, and they are not each other, then we are right back to three gods, no matter how similar they are in their will, actions, attributes, et cetera. Well, there could be another option. Maybe the statement “is God” is understood as in they are parts of God. This is actually the solution of a popular Christian apologist, William Lane Craig, in an attempt to avoid the problem of tritheism. However, this view is clearly unorthodox partialism, as none of the persons is said to be fully God. Okay, well, if we can't find a solution like that, what if we just say all seven premises are true, and we just accept that true contradictions can exist? This has recently been proposed by American philosopher J. C. Beal. But again, this violates the law of non contradiction or it violates logic. So this cannot be considered a solution from the paradigm of classical logic. Okay, well, what if we say all seven premises are true, but they're only apparently contradictory, not actually contradictory, even if we can't tell you why or how, they aren't actually contradictory? This is a form of mysterianism. Or it's an appeal to mystery, which has recently been proposed by James Anderson. This is not a solution to the problem. Rather, it's questioning whether a solution is required from an epistemological standpoint, how do we determine when something is actually contradictory versus when it's apparently contradictory? So, given this brief explanation of the logical problem of the Trinity and its responses, we are left with either modalism, denying classical identity, partialism, tritheism denying the law of non contradiction, or mysterianism, which argues that the LPT may be unsolvable. But Christians are still justified in affirming the doctrine of the trinity.
