# TELLS — App Store Listing

## App Name

TELLS — Spot the Liar

## Subtitle

Social Intelligence Training Disguised as a Game

## App Store Description

**Everyone's lying to you. You just can't see it yet.**

Your friend says they're "fine." Your coworker says "no worries." Your date says they "don't usually do this." You believe all of them.

TELLS changes that.

**Watch four people answer the same question in a group chat. One is lying. The others are telling the truth. You have to figure out who.**

But here's what makes this different from every other game on your phone: the lies are constructed using real behavioral science. The liar answers faster. Their words are vaguer. They skip the embarrassing details that real stories always include. The truth-tellers pause at painful memories. They correct themselves mid-sentence. They include details that make them look bad.

You won't notice any of this at first. But after ten sessions, you'll start seeing it everywhere — in texts, in meetings, in conversations you used to take at face value.

**Five training modes. One skill that changes everything.**

🔍 **READ** — Watch messages type out in real-time with pauses, revisions, and speed changes. Spot the liar by reading their behavioral patterns.

🧠 **WHY** — Someone says something. Figure out the real reason behind it. (Spoiler: it's almost never what they said.)

💎 **FEEL** — "I'm fine." They're not. Name the exact emotion they're hiding.

🪞 **BENEATH** — Read the subtext. What are they actually telling you without saying it?

🕊️ **DEFUSE** — A conflict escalates in real-time. Your choices compound. Pick wrong and it gets worse. Pick right and you learn something you'll use this week.

⚔️ **CRAFT** — Build your response piece by piece. Three slots, multiple options per slot. Your combination gets graded on social strategy — including principles from the 48 Laws of Power.

**What players say after two weeks:**

"I caught my friend lying about why she cancelled and I didn't even mean to notice."

"My manager does the 'no rush' thing and now I can't unsee it."

"I used the DEFUSE technique at Thanksgiving. It actually worked."

**Your profile tracks 10 social intelligence skills** — from Timing Sensitivity to Emotional Granularity — so you can see exactly which dimensions of reading people you're strong at and where your blind spots are. Session reports show before/after skill changes. A radar chart visualizes your full profile.

**30+ named behavioral principles** you'll internalize through gameplay, not lectures. Principles like Temporal Bridging, Diplomatic Aggression, Borrowed Enthusiasm, Redemption Coda, and Rehearsed Indifference.

Daily challenges rotate modes so you build across all dimensions. Streaks reward consistency. Share cards let you challenge friends with your score grid — like Wordle, but for reading people.

**The behavioral science is the gameplay. Not a feature. Not a lesson. The game.**

You'll never read a text message the same way again.

# SWAY — App Store Listing

## App Name

SWAY — The Influence Game

## Subtitle

Learn What Makes People Say Yes

## App Store Description

**You're being influenced right now.** The font. The layout. The fact that you've read this far. That wasn't an accident.

SWAY teaches you to see it — and to do it.

**This is a game about the invisible mechanics of persuasion, negotiation, and social power.** Every scenario is pulled from real life: salary negotiations, difficult conversations, sales interactions, social dynamics. Every answer is graded on actual influence science — framing effects, compliance psychology, pre-suasion, and strategic communication.

You won't study any of this. You'll play it. And then you'll notice it in every conversation, email, and meeting for the rest of your life.

**Six modes. Six dimensions of influence.**

🌊 **PACE** — Before you can move anyone, you have to meet them where they are. Read their emotional state and match it. Get this wrong and nothing else works.

🧭 **LEAD** — Now shift them. Your friend is stuck in resentment. Your team is spiraling. Move them from State A to State B using the Validate → Evidence → Action sequence that therapists, negotiators, and the best managers all use without naming it.

🪞 **FRAME** — Same facts. Different framing. Completely different decision. Learn why "we're losing 5 hours a week" lands and "this saves 5 hours a week" doesn't. This is Kahneman in your pocket.

🔗 **CHAIN** — Build a yes-set. Three micro-agreements that make your final ask feel like the obvious next step. You pick the opener, the bridge, and the close. Each combination scored. This is how the best salespeople, fundraisers, and negotiators operate — and now you'll see the architecture.

⚡ **BREAK** — Now play defense. A salesperson stacks scarcity, social proof, and time pressure on you in one sentence. A friend guilt-trips with "no pressure." A negotiator manufactures a debt you never agreed to. Identify the technique and it loses its power.

🌀 **SWAY** — Full influence scenarios combining everything. Read the room. Pace. Reframe. Close. This is the final exam — and the reason people keep playing.

**Here's what changes after you play for a week:**

Your salary negotiation stops being about what you deserve and starts being about external anchors.

"No rush" from your manager stops meaning "no rush."

Your friend's "I'm fine" stops being an answer and starts being data.

The "no pressure" text from someone who wants something stops working on you.

**Your Influence Profile tracks six skills:** Pacing, Leading, Framing, Compliance, Resistance, and Calibration. Each session adjusts your profile based on what you got right and what you missed. Blind spot analysis tells you where you're vulnerable. Technique cards build a personal library of influence patterns you've learned to recognize and deploy.

**Progression from Unread → Observer → Mirror → Framer → Operator → Influence → Svengali.** Not based on time played. Based on demonstrated skill.

Named techniques include Pace Before Lead, Tilt the Lens, External Anchor, Status Quo as Loss, Compliance Momentum, Pre-suasion, Name It to Tame It, and Unrequested Sacrifice.

Daily challenges. Streaks. Shareable results.

**The game uses the same techniques it teaches.** The progression system is a commitment escalation. The daily challenge is a variable reward schedule. The share cards are social proof. If you noticed that — you're already learning.

Most people won't notice. That's why they need this game.

# Notes for the Developer

## Persuasion Architecture Used in These Descriptions

Both descriptions practice what they preach. Here's what's embedded:

**TELLS description:**

- **Loss framing** in the opener — "Everyone's lying to you" frames the status quo as a loss
- **Identity play** — "You believe all of them" creates dissonance with who the reader wants to be
- **Specificity as proof** — Named principles (Temporal Bridging, Diplomatic Aggression) function as credibility signals, same way truth-tellers use specific details
- **Social proof through testimonials** — The three quotes are crafted to show behavioral change, not satisfaction
- **Future pacing** — "You'll never read a text message the same way again" makes the post-purchase state vivid
- **Pattern interrupt** — "The behavioral science is the gameplay. Not a feature. Not a lesson. The game." uses repetition structure to break scanning behavior

**SWAY description:**

- **Meta-awareness hook** — "You're being influenced right now" is a pattern interrupt that also demonstrates the product
- **The game uses itself** — Explicitly naming the influence techniques used in the description (commitment escalation, variable rewards, social proof) is a trust-building transparency move that paradoxically makes the techniques more effective, not less
- **Loss framing** — Every "what changes" bullet reframes something the reader already experiences as a loss they didn't know they had
- **Commitment escalation** — The description is structured so each section deepens investment. By the time they reach the download button, they've already committed attention, curiosity, and self-concept
- **Identity close** — "Most people won't notice. That's why they need this game." positions downloading as proof of being the person who notices

Both descriptions avoid:

- Feature lists (boring, skimmable, forgettable)
- Comparison to competitors (validates their existence)
- Price justification (frames the purchase as a cost instead of an investment)
- Excessive exclamation marks (signals desperation)
