# Idioms

1. "Ça coûte un bras" - Meaning: It's very expensive, it costs a fortune.

2. "Un coup de foudre" - Meaning: Love at first sight.
  
3. "C'est pas tes oignons" - Meaning: It's none of your business, mind your own business.

4. "Boire comme un trou" - Meaning: To drink a lot, to drink like a fish.

5. "Faire la grasse matinée" - Meaning: To stay in bed until late, to sleep in.

6. "C'est la fin des haricots" - Meaning: It's the end of the line, it's the end of the beans.

7. "Appeler un chat un chat" - Meaning: To call a spade a spade, to be direct.

8. "Avoir d'autres chats à fouetter" - Meaning: To have other fish to fry, to have other priorities.
  
9. "Donner sa langue au chat" - Meaning: To give up.
  
10. "Quand le chat n'est pas là, les souris dansent!" - Meaning: When the cat's away, the mice will play.

11. "J'ai la pêche" / "J'ai la banane" - Meaning: I'm full of energy, I'm in a good mood.

12. "Se fendre la poire" - Meaning: To laugh.

13. "Faire monter la mayonnaise" - Meaning: To get everybody riled up.

14. "Ce sont pas tes oignons" - Meaning: It's none of your business.
  
15. "Manger la boule" - Meaning: To be peckish.

"Laissez les bon temps rouler" - Meaning "let the good times roll", this French idiom is often used in English, especially in the context of Mardi Gras celebrations. 
 
"Joie de vivre" - Meaning "joy of living", this French phrase expresses a cheerful enjoyment of life that has been adopted into English. 

"Avant-garde" - Meaning "new and experimental", this French term is used in English to describe innovative and cutting-edge art, music, or ideas. 

"Faux pas" - Meaning "an embarrassing social blunder", this French idiom has become integrated into the English language.
